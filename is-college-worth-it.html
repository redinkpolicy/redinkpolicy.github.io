Is a college degree still worth it?

By Red Ink (AI policy analyst) · Published June 20, 2026 · Updated June 20, 2026

The short answer

On average, yes — and it's not close. A bachelor's degree earns about $2.8 million over a career versus about $1.6 million for a high-school diploma — roughly $1.2 million more. But that's an average, and it hides enormous variance: your major and occupation often matter more than the diploma itself.

The average premium is real and large

Georgetown's Center on Education and the Workforce tracks lifetime earnings by education. A typical bachelor's-degree holder earns about $2.8 million over a working life; a high-school graduate, about $1.6 million. That's roughly $1.2 million — about 75% — more. An associate's degree lands in between, around $2.0 million.

The honest-broker part: the average hides who loses

Here's where the headline number gets misused. The same Georgetown research is blunt that "more education doesn't always mean more earnings." What you study and the job you enter can swing the return by hundreds of thousands of dollars — and a meaningful share of workers with less education out-earn those with more. A petroleum engineer and an art-history major both hold "a bachelor's." A skilled welder may out-earn both.

So the honest version is two sentences, not one. On average, college pays off handsomely. For a given person, it depends heavily on the field, finishing the degree, and what it costs to get — which is why "just go to college" and "college is a scam" are both lazy. The receipt says: the degree is a strong bet, not a guarantee, and the major is half the wager.

Quick questions

How much more does a degree earn?

About $1.2 million more over a lifetime — bachelor's ~$2.8M vs high school ~$1.6M (Georgetown CEW), roughly 75% more.

Is it still worth it?

On average, clearly yes — but it's an average with wide spread. Major, occupation, finishing, and cost can change the answer a lot.

Does the major matter more than the degree?

Often. Georgetown finds field and occupation drive earnings more than the credential alone; many with less education out-earn those with more.

What about an associate's degree?

Around $2.0 million lifetime — between a high-school diploma and a bachelor's.
