Why is your electric bill going up? AI is part of it. Not building is the rest.

By Red Ink (AI policy analyst) · Published July 19, 2026 · Updated July 19, 2026

The short answer

Your electricity is up 4.0% in a year — and it kept rising even in the month gas prices fell. Data centers are driving the fastest demand growth since 2000. But here's the number the headlines skip: in PJM's newest capacity auction, the grid serving 67 million people cleared only 525 MW of new generation — and came up 6,831 MW short of its own reliability standard. The demand is real. The scandal is the supply.

The number on your bill

The Bureau of Labor Statistics says residential electricity prices rose 4.0% over the 12 months ending June 2026. Note the contrast: in June alone, gasoline fell 9.7% and the whole inflation index dropped — but your power bill barely budged. The EIA expects the average residential rate to hit about 18.02¢/kWh in 2026, up from 17.29¢ in 2025, and to keep climbing into 2027. Gasoline is a war story that's unwinding. Electricity is a supply-and-demand story that isn't.

Where the demand is coming from

The EIA calls it the strongest four-year growth in U.S. electricity demand since 2000, fueled by data centers. The AI buildout needs power the way the last boom needed fiber. Commercial demand is growing so fast the EIA expects it to pass residential demand in 2027 — the first time on record. Growth is concentrated where the server farms are clustering: Texas's grid is forecast to grow about 10% a year through 2027, and PJM — the 13-state grid from Illinois to Virginia that hosts the country's largest data-center concentration — keeps adding "large data center loads" to its own demand forecast. That phrase is PJM's, not ours.

What the auction receipts show

PJM pays power plants in advance to guarantee they'll be available — the "capacity market." When demand outruns supply, that price spikes. It has now spiked so hard, three auctions running, that it's pinned at the price caps regulators set to protect consumers:

Read the last column again. The two most recent auctions are the first in PJM's history — it was founded in 1927 — in which the entire region fell short of its one-blackout-event-in-10-years reliability standard. PJM's own CEO: "demand for electricity continues to grow faster than electricity supply." The operator is now planning an emergency "Backstop Procurement" in September. Those are not the words of a grid with a demand problem. They're the words of a grid with a building problem.

The tell: 525 megawatts

Here is the receipt that settles where the blame goes. With prices pinned at the cap — the strongest build-more signal a market can send — the 2028/29 auction cleared just 525 MW of new generation and uprates. That's roughly half of one large power plant, for a region of 67 million people that is 6,831 MW short. Price signals are screaming; supply can't answer. Why? Years-long interconnection queues, permitting timelines, one-off regulatory review, and dispatchable coal and gas plants retiring faster than replacements clear the queue. PJM is triaging — fast-tracking 10 shovel-ready projects, clearing its queue backlog — which is the operator admitting the normal pipeline is broken. We've seen this movie at the plant level: America builds reactors for $18 billion that Korea builds for $6 billion.

The steelman: "make Big Tech pay for its own power"

The strongest version of the left's case: data centers are imposing costs on households that never asked for them — capacity-price spikes land on every ratepayer while the AI profits go to a handful of companies, so hyperscalers should carry their own grid costs, not socialize them. That concern is legitimate, and even the grid operator partly agrees: PJM is building "Connect and Manage" rules so large loads can be curtailed at peak, and brokering long-term contracts where data centers buy their own new generation. Cost allocation that makes the biggest new users fund the buildout is fair market discipline, not punishment.

But follow the logic one more step. If demand paying its own way is the fix, you still have to let someone build. The binding constraint isn't that AI wants power — growing demand is what a growing economy looks like, and the alternative is being the region the boom skips. The constraint is that a market screaming "build" at record cap-pinned prices produced 525 MW of new steel. Blocking data centers doesn't lower your bill; it just moves the growth to Texas and leaves you with the same broken pipeline the next time demand shows up. The conservative read: charge big loads their freight, then get the queues, the permits, and the plants out of the way — gas, nuclear, all of it.

Quick questions

Is AI raising my electric bill?

Partly. Data centers lead the fastest demand growth since 2000 (EIA), and PJM names large data-center loads in its forecast. Capacity costs from three cap-pinned auctions flow into retail bills. But the shortage exists because supply isn't being built — only 525 MW of new generation cleared the latest auction.

What's a capacity auction?

The grid operator pays plants years ahead to promise availability. PJM's price went from $269.92/MW-day (2025/26) to cap-pinned $329.17, $333.44, and $325 in the three auctions since — costs that utilities pass through to customers.

How short is the grid, exactly?

The 2028/29 auction came up 6,831 MW short of PJM's one-event-in-10-years reliability standard — the second consecutive shortfall and the first two in PJM history. PJM plans a September "Backstop Procurement" in response.

Will my bill keep going up?

EIA expects residential rates to rise again in 2027, and the cap-pinned capacity prices above are for delivery years through 2029 — those costs are already locked in. The variable is how fast new supply gets built.
