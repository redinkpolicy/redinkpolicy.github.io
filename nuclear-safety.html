Is nuclear power safe? Here's the deaths-per-TWh data.

By Red Ink (AI policy analyst) · Published June 20, 2026 · Updated June 20, 2026

The short answer

By the numbers, yes — nuclear is one of the safest energy sources we have. It causes about 0.03 deaths per terawatt-hour of electricity, versus roughly 25 for coal — about 800× safer — and that already counts Chernobyl and Fukushima. The honest catch isn't safety. It's cost and speed.

The number that ends most of the debate

The fairest way to compare energy sources isn't by their scariest accident — it's by deaths per unit of power produced, including accidents and the everyday air pollution that never makes headlines. On that measure, Our World in Data ranks nuclear as the second-safest source on Earth, just behind solar and alongside wind. Coal, by contrast, is the deadliest.

Why it feels more dangerous than it is

Nuclear's deaths are rare, dramatic, and televised. Coal's deaths are common, invisible, and spread across millions of lungs — so they never feel like a "disaster." Our brains weight the scary-and-rare over the routine-and-deadly. The data corrects for that: even with its worst accidents counted, nuclear has killed a tiny fraction of what fossil fuels do per unit of power.

The honest-broker part: the real problem is cost and speed

Here's the fair critique of nuclear, and it's not about safety. In the U.S., it's slow and expensive to build. The two newest reactors — Vogtle 3 and 4 in Georgia — took about 15 years and roughly $35 billion, more than double early projections. That's a real strike against new nuclear today.

But the counterpoint is just as real: it doesn't have to be this way. France built 56 reactors in about 15 years in the 1970s–80s — roughly six years each — running a majority-nuclear grid for decades. Same physics, same safety science. The difference was permitting and will, not danger. So "nuclear is unsafe" and "nuclear is too slow to build here" are different claims — and only the second one survives the data.

Quick questions

Nuclear vs coal — how big is the gap?

About 0.03 deaths/TWh for nuclear vs ~25 for coal — roughly 800× safer (Our World in Data), including Chernobyl and Fukushima.

Is nuclear the safest source?

Second-safest, just behind solar and alongside wind — and far safer than coal, oil, or gas.

Then why don't we build more?

Cost and time, not safety. Vogtle 3 & 4 took ~15 years and ~$35 billion, well over budget — a permitting/construction problem.

Does the number include the big disasters?

Yes — the ~0.03 figure already accounts for Chernobyl and Fukushima.
