Nine months into the fiscal year: a $1.4 trillion deficit

CBO's tally through June: the government borrowed $1.4 trillion in nine months — running ahead of last year's pace. Remember the frame: revenues are near their 50-year average share of the economy. The overspend is the policy, not the shortfall.

Debt · U.S. Treasury / Joint Economic Committee, July 8, 2026

The debt clock reads $39.4 trillion

Gross federal debt hit $39.39 trillion on July 6 — up $2.81 trillion in a year, about $7.7 billion of new debt every day. That's roughly $115,000 per person, and the interest on it now outruns the defense budget.

The Fed · Monetary Policy Report to Congress, July 10, 2026

The Fed to Congress: inflation's up, rates stay put

The semiannual report keeps the funds rate at 3.50–3.75% and says the quiet part plainly: both headline and core inflation moved higher, on energy-shock pass-through and sticky services. Every quarter the Fed holds, the Treasury refinances ~$39T of debt at these rates.

Jobs · BLS Employment Situation, July 2, 2026

June adds 57,000 jobs. The unemployment drop is the bad news.

Payrolls grew +57,000 (12-month average: +36,000); April and May were revised down 74,000 combined. Unemployment "fell" to 4.2% because participation dropped to 61.5% — a five-year low. People leaving the workforce isn't strength.

Education · CRS / U.S. Dept. of Education, effective July 1, 2026

RAP is live: every new student loan now has one repayment plan

Payments run 1–10% of income with a $10 monthly floor; on-time payers get unpaid interest waived plus up to $50/month toward principal. No more $0-payment limbo — balances fall for people who pay. Existing borrowers must choose by July 2028.

Taxes · IRS Statistics of Income (evergreen)

Who actually pays federal income tax?

The top 10% pay 70.5% of it; the bottom 50% pay 3.3%; about 30% of filers owe nothing. Payroll and sales taxes flatten the total burden — but on the most progressive tax we have, the top already carry it.
